Week 1 History & Objective

Where it all started — and what a DJ actually does.

DJing was born in the late 1960s and exploded in the 1970s. DJ Kool Herc is widely credited as the father of hip-hop DJing — he discovered the "merry-go-round" technique, isolating the drum break of a record and looping it to keep the party going. Grandmaster Flash refined this into "quick-mix theory," while Frankie Knuckles built the sound of house music at The Warehouse in Chicago. In Europe, disco and electronic dance music gave rise to a different but parallel DJ culture.

Over decades, DJing evolved from party tool to art form to career. Today, DJs perform in clubs, arenas, radio booths, weddings, and corporate events worldwide.

The Objective

A DJ's job is simple on paper: keep the dance floor moving. In practice, that means reading the crowd, building energy over time, choosing the right track at the right moment, and connecting one song to the next so seamlessly that people don't notice — they just feel it.

Every technical skill you learn — beat matching, harmonic mixing, phrasing — is a tool in service of that one goal. The crowd is always the real instrument.

Week 1 What is Spinning

Playing records as a performance instrument.

"Spinning" refers to the act of playing recorded music — originally vinyl records on turntables — in a way that creates a continuous, curated musical experience. Unlike a musician who plays notes, a DJ plays tracks. But that's only the beginning.

Turntablism elevated spinning into its own art form: using the turntable as an instrument through scratching, beat juggling, and manipulation of the playback. Even if you're mixing on CDJs or a controller today, understanding the physical mechanics of a turntable gives you a deeper intuition for tempo, feel, and timing.

Vinyl → CDJ → Controller

The tools have changed, but the principles haven't. Vinyl taught DJs to feel music physically. CDJs digitized that feel. Controllers made it portable. The ear training that matters most is the same across all three.

When a DJ "spins," they're making real-time decisions — not just pressing play. They're controlling tempo, volume, EQ, effects, and timing simultaneously while watching the crowd and thinking three tracks ahead.

Week 2 BPM & Beat Matching

Sync two tracks. Keep the floor moving.

BPM (Beats Per Minute) is the tempo of a track — how many beats occur in one minute. A 128 BPM house track has 128 beats every 60 seconds. Most DJ software and hardware can detect BPM automatically, but learning to hear it is fundamental.

Slow
70–100
Hip-hop, R&B, slow jams
Mid
110–125
Pop, disco, nu-disco
House
124–132
House, tech house, garage
Fast
138–160+
Techno, trance, drum & bass

Beat matching is the process of aligning the BPM of two tracks so they play simultaneously without one running ahead or behind the other. Before sync buttons existed, DJs did this by ear using the pitch fader — a slider that slightly speeds up or slows down a track — and nudging the platter to push a beat forward or backward in real time.

How to beat match manually

1. Play Track A through the speakers. 2. Cue up Track B in your headphones. 3. Tap the BPM of both tracks. 4. Adjust Track B's pitch fader until the tempos feel identical. 5. Nudge the platter until the beats align. 6. Bring Track B into the mix.

Even if you use sync, train your ear first. DJs who can't beat match manually are vulnerable the moment the technology fails — and they'll never fully understand why their mixes sound off.

Week 2 EQ & Filter

Shaping sound — lows, mids, highs & the filter.

Every DJ mixer has an EQ (equalizer) for each channel — typically three knobs labeled Low, Mid, and High. These let you boost or cut specific frequency ranges of a track in real time. Understanding EQ is what separates a DJ who plays songs from one who actually mixes them.

Low
20–300 Hz
Kick drum, bass, sub frequencies. The thump and weight of a track.
Mid
300 Hz–4 kHz
Vocals, synths, snares, guitars. The body and presence of a track.
High
4–20 kHz
Hi-hats, cymbals, air, brightness. The sparkle and sheen on top.

During a mix, you use EQ to prevent clashing between two tracks playing simultaneously. The most common technique is the bass swap — cutting the lows on the incoming track while the outgoing track is still playing, then gradually bringing the new bass in as you fade the old one out. This prevents two kick drums from doubling up and muddying the low end.

The bass swap — step by step

1. Cut the Low EQ on Track B all the way down before bringing it in.
2. Begin your crossfade — Track B is now audible in mids and highs only.
3. Gradually cut the lows on Track A while simultaneously raising the lows on Track B.
4. Once Track B's bass is fully in, cut Track A out entirely.

The Mid EQ is useful for managing vocals — if both tracks have prominent vocals, cutting the mids on one can reduce the clash. The High EQ is subtler; slight cuts can make a mix feel warmer, while a boost adds excitement and brightness.

The Filter

The filter is a separate effect — usually a single knob — that sweeps all frequencies at once. Turn it one way and it becomes a low-pass filter (cuts the highs, leaving only warm bass and mids). Turn it the other way and it becomes a high-pass filter (cuts the lows, leaving only bright, thin frequencies). DJs use filters to create tension and build energy — filtering a track down to just bass creates a hypnotic, raw feel; filtering up to just highs creates the sensation of something about to drop.

EQ and filter decisions should be musical, not mechanical. Listen first, then adjust. The goal is always a clean, balanced sound — two tracks playing together should feel like one.

Week 2 Structure

Phrases, beats & bars — the grammar of music.

Music is structured in a predictable hierarchy. Understanding this structure lets you make transitions that feel natural instead of jarring — because you're working with the music, not against it.

Beat
1
The single pulse. The "thump" of the kick drum.
Bar
4 beats
4 beats = 1 bar. The basic unit of rhythm.
Phrase
4–8 bars
A musical sentence. Where the energy shifts.
Section
16–32 bars
Verse, chorus, drop, breakdown — the big blocks.

In most dance music, energy changes happen at phrase boundaries — every 16 or 32 bars. A breakdown drops at bar 64. A drop hits at bar 96. If you start a transition mid-phrase, it will feel wrong even to listeners who can't explain why.

The rule

Always start and end your mixes on phrase boundaries — ideally at the top of an 8 or 16-bar phrase. Count in your head: 1-2-3-4, 2-2-3-4, 3-2-3-4… Listen for the "1" to hit with the musical change.

Phrasing is the single most overlooked skill in beginner DJs — and the single most obvious tell when it's wrong. Get this right and your mixes will immediately sound more professional.

Week 2 Harmonic Mixing

Song keys & the Camelot Wheel.

Every song is written in a musical key — a set of notes that the melody, chords, and bassline are built around. When two tracks in incompatible keys are mixed together, the result is a clash that sounds dissonant, even "wrong," to listeners.

Harmonic mixing is the practice of only mixing tracks whose keys are musically compatible — so the transition sounds melodically smooth. The tool that makes this practical for DJs is the Camelot Wheel.

The Camelot Wheel maps all 24 musical keys (12 major, 12 minor) onto a clock-like grid. Each position has a number (1–12) and a letter (A = minor, B = major). Compatible keys are adjacent on the wheel.

The 3 safe moves

Same number, switch letter — e.g. 8A → 8B (relative major/minor, very smooth).
+1 or −1 on the number — e.g. 8A → 9A or 7A (adjacent key, energy up or down).
Same number, same letter — mixing two tracks in the exact same key (always safe).

Most DJ software (Rekordbox, Serato, Traktor) can detect and display Camelot keys automatically. Tag your music library — it turns harmonic mixing from guesswork into a system.

Week 3 Track Formats

Radio edits vs. extended versions.

The same song can exist in multiple formats — and the format matters a great deal to a DJ. Choosing the wrong version can leave you scrambling during a live mix.

Format Length Structure DJ Use
Radio Edit ~3–4 min Intro → verse → chorus → outro. Often starts immediately on the vocal. Outro cuts quickly. Tough to mix in/out of. Use for background music or a very quick-drop effect.
Extended Version ~6–8 min Long instrumental intro + outro. Minimal arrangement in the mix sections — space to blend. Ideal for DJing. The long intro/outro is your mixing window. You have time to blend properly.
Club Mix / Remix ~6–10 min Aggressive drop, stripped-down arrangement. Built specifically for the floor. Best for peak-hour sets. Often more energetic than the original.
Acapella / Instrumental varies Vocals only or music only. For mashups, layering vocals over another track's instrumental.

Rule of thumb

When building your library, always hunt for the extended version first. It gives you the most control. You can always start a mix later into the track — you can't add intro space that isn't there.

Week 3 The Set

Creating playlists & reading the crowd.

A DJ set is a journey. The job isn't to play your favorite songs in a row — it's to take a crowd somewhere and bring them back. That requires an energy arc: a deliberate rise, peak, and resolution over the course of the set.

Opening
Low
Warm up the room. Familiar grooves, mid-tempo, nothing too heavy.
Build
Rising
Introduce tension. Slightly faster, bigger drops, crowd starts moving.
Peak
High
Your biggest weapons. Full energy. The crowd is yours.
Cool Down
Falling
Bring it back. Last song is slower — leave them wanting more.

Crowd reading is the skill of adjusting your plan in real time based on what you see. Is the floor filling? Good — keep building. Is it thinning out? You might have pushed too hard too early — drop back, rebuild. The pre-planned playlist is a guide, not a script.

Signals to watch for

Arms raised, singing along — they know this track, they love it. Let it breathe.
People checking their phones — you may have lost the room. Pivot now.
Moving toward the floor — energy is building. Don't cut it short.
Moving to the bar — the last track didn't land. Make a move.

Build your playlist in zones, not individual tracks. A zone might be 20 minutes of tech-house at 128 BPM in 8A/9A keys — but within that zone, you have 8–10 tracks to choose from depending on what the room needs. Flexibility within structure is what separates a good DJ from a great one.

Your library is your most important asset. Organize it well — by BPM, key, energy level, and genre. The DJ who knows their music wins every time.
